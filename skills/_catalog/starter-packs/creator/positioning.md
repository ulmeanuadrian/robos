# Positioning

## One-Liner
I share what actually works for growing as a creator -- the real numbers,
the real process, the real mistakes -- not the polished highlight reel.

## Value Proposition
You get the advice I wish someone had given me when I started. Not
recycled "post consistently and be authentic" platitudes, but specific
strategies I've tested myself, with receipts. I show my actual analytics,
share my actual revenue, and tell you about the things that flopped
just as much as the things that worked.

What the audience gets:
- Tested strategies with real data behind them (not "trust me bro")
- Behind-the-scenes of what running a creator business actually looks like
- Permission to do things differently than the "guru" playbook
- A creator who responds to comments and treats the audience like peers
- Regular content that's worth their time, not filler for the algorithm

## Differentiators
1. **Radical transparency.** I share real numbers -- revenue, views, costs,
   failures. Not vague "6-figure creator" claims. Actual spreadsheets.
   This builds trust that no amount of polished content can match.
2. **Builds in public.** The audience watches projects develop in real
   time -- from idea to execution to results (or failure). They learn
   by watching the process, not just the recap.
3. **Anti-guru.** I don't sell a dream or promise a formula. I share
   what worked for me and let the audience adapt it. No upsell funnels
   disguised as free content.
4. **Platform-native.** Each platform gets content made for that platform.
   The newsletter isn't a repurposed YouTube script. The tweet isn't a
   chopped-up blog post. Respects the medium.
5. **Niche depth over breadth.** Deep expertise in [specific topic]
   rather than shallow takes on everything. The go-to person for this
   one thing, not a general "creator tips" account.

## Competitor Landscape
- **Big creator gurus** (Ali Abdaal, Pat Flynn type): polished, systematic,
  often focused on productivity/business. You're positioned as: more
  accessible, more raw, more niche-specific. Same principles but from
  someone at a more relatable stage.
- **Niche competitors**: other creators in the same topic space.
  Differentiate on personality, transparency, and unique angle. You
  don't need to be better at everything -- just different enough that
  some people prefer your take.
- **Courses and communities** (Cohort-based courses, paid communities):
  structured but expensive and time-intensive. You're positioned as:
  the free/low-cost alternative that delivers 80% of the value through
  content alone.
- **General social media advice** (Social media examiners, marketing blogs):
  broad and generic. You're positioned as: creator-first, not
  marketer-first. Advice from someone who creates content, not someone
  who studies content creation.

## Category
Creator education through practice. Not a course seller. Not a coach.
A working creator who teaches by sharing their process publicly. The
category is "creator who teaches" not "teacher who creates."

## Proof Points
Customize with real data:
- Audience size: "X subscribers/followers across platforms"
- Growth trajectory: "grew from X to Y in Z months" (with context)
- Revenue transparency: "made $X last year from [breakdown]"
- Content track record: "X videos/newsletters/posts published"
- Notable collaborations or features
- Community engagement: "average X comments per post" or response rate
- Specific wins: "video that got X views" or "newsletter with Y% open rate"
- Audience testimonials: quotes from real followers about what they learned
