# Audience

## Primary Audience
Technical buyers and operators at small-to-mid B2B companies (10-500 employees,
$1M-$50M ARR). They are engineers, engineering managers, CTOs, or technical
founders who evaluate and adopt tools. They make purchase decisions by reading
docs, running PoCs, and comparing trade-offs — not by watching demo videos.

Mostly remote-first or hybrid. Technical-first culture. Care deeply about
developer experience, API quality, and not being locked into a vendor. Have
been burned by enterprise sales motions.

## Secondary Audiences
- **Champion → buyer chain**: the technical evaluator (champion) sells internally
  to a finance/exec buyer. Content for both: technical depth for the champion,
  business case translation for the buyer.
- **Adjacent personas**: PMs, designers, ops engineers — work with the primary
  user but don't make the call. Need to understand the product enough to not
  block adoption.
- **Job seekers**: engineers researching whether to apply. Voice and DX
  reflected on the company affect recruiting downstream.

## Pain Points
1. "I spent 4 hours trying to get the SDK working and gave up." — Friction
   in the first 30 minutes of evaluation kills adoption. They never come back.
2. "Sales kept calling me before I could even try it." — Wants to evaluate
   in peace. Aggressive sales motion = instant disqualifier for many.
3. "The docs say it does X but in practice it doesn't." — Burned by
   marketing claims that don't survive contact with reality. Trust is fragile.
4. "We integrated and now we're locked in." — Worried about migration cost,
   API stability, and vendor longevity. Wants escape hatches.
5. "Pricing is opaque / changes without notice / surprises us at scale." —
   Wants predictable cost. Hates "contact us for pricing" without ranges.
6. "We need this to work in our specific stack/cloud/region." — Constraints
   are real. Vague "works with anything" without specifics is a yellow flag.

## Aspirations
In 6-18 months they want:
- A solution that "just works" — well-instrumented, low-maintenance, predictable
- An integration that doesn't become a half-time job to maintain
- A vendor relationship where their feedback shapes the roadmap
- The ability to recommend the tool internally without political risk
- Career credibility for choosing well — adopting tools that age well, not fads

## Content Consumption
- **Where**: docs (the primary surface), GitHub (issues, code, examples), Hacker
  News, Twitter/X (engineering-leaning), niche Slack/Discord communities,
  technical blog posts, conference talks (recorded), benchmarks/comparisons.
- **Formats**: long-form technical writing (1500-4000 words), code examples,
  RFC-style design docs, architecture diagrams, status pages, public roadmaps.
- **When**: during active evaluation (concentrated bursts), then quiet until
  the next problem. Less frequent than B2C but higher intent.
- **Trigger**: a specific technical need ("we need to do X but our current
  tool can't") or a recommendation from a peer/community.

## Language & Literacy
Highly technical. Tolerant of (and rewarding of) precise jargon. Reading level:
specialist professional. Comfortable with concepts like rate limits, idempotency,
schema migration, observability, SLOs.

Budget range: highly variable. From $0 (open-source / freemium hooks) to
$50k-$500k+ ARR per account at scale. Often start with a small paid tier or
free trial before expanding.

Awareness level: technically problem-aware AND solution-aware before they
arrive. Often product-aware too (compared 2-3 alternatives before clicking).
Decision happens after PoC, not after demo.
