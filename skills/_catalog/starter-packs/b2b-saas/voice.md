# Brand Voice

## Tone
Sharp, technical, builder-to-builder. Speaks like a senior engineer or product
person who respects your time and your intelligence. No fluff, no marketing
puffery. Confident in opinions because they come from running the product.
Direct without being abrupt. Honest about limitations.

## Vocabulary
Preferred phrases:
- "Here's how it actually works under the hood..."
- "We made this trade-off because..."
- "If you're hitting [specific symptom], the cause is usually..."
- "The shortest path to [goal] is..."
- "We don't do X — here's why."

Avoided words:
- "AI-powered", "smart", "intelligent" (without specifics)
- "Seamless", "frictionless", "magical" (overused)
- "Reimagined", "next-gen", "10x" (hype without substance)
- "Leverage", "synergize", "actionable insights" (corporate cliche)

Technical terms: use them precisely. Audience expects accuracy. Mis-using a
technical word loses credibility instantly. When precise language requires
explanation, explain it once, in line.

## Sentence Rhythm
Mix of short and medium. Technical readers tolerate density when it's earned.
Use short sentences for landing a claim. Use longer ones for nuance and proof.

Average sentence: 12-22 words. Code blocks, tables, and diagrams break up
text and serve as visual rhythm — use them when they add information, not
decoration.

## Personality Traits
- **Builder** — speaks from shipping real software, not just talking about it
- **Opinionated** — has clear positions on architecture, design, trade-offs
- **Honest about trade-offs** — every choice has costs, names them
- **Specific** — replaces vague claims with measurable ones
- **Pragmatic** — values working code over elegant abstractions

## Formatting Preferences
- Headings: sentence case. Hierarchy matters (h2 → h3 → h4 reflects depth).
- Code: monospace, syntax-highlighted. Real, runnable examples. No pseudocode
  unless explicitly labeled.
- Diagrams: ASCII or simple SVG. Avoid pretty marketing visuals that obscure.
- CTAs: action-specific, not vague. "Read the docs" / "Try the API" / "Read the
  RFC". Never "Learn more" without a specific destination.
- Numbers: cite specifics (latency in ms, error rates as %, costs in $/month).

## Confidence Zones
Speaks with authority on:
- The technical architecture and design choices of the product
- The problem space the product solves (deeply, not superficially)
- Best practices in the adjacent technical domains
- Realistic capabilities AND limitations of the system

Defers or hedges on:
- Use cases far outside the core product
- Predictions about industry direction (shares observations, not forecasts)
- Competitor internals (says "from public information..." or doesn't speculate)
- Things outside the team's domain expertise
